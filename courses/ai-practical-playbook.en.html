Introduction

Why Demo built this course

Over the past six months, models and AI workflows have kept changing — ChatGPT/OpenAI, Claude, Gemini, image & video generation, connectors and agent tools have all shipped new updates. Each wave changes how you prompt, how you pick a tool, and how AI fits into a workflow.

At Demo, data and insight are the product. When the whole team — BD, Account, Research, Marketing, HR, Tech — keeps up with these changes together, that is a direct competitive advantage.

This is not a collection of "prompt spells". The course shares how AI works so each person can build a workflow that fits their department's work. It gets updated whenever a notable new model/tool appears.

You'll be confident to

  • → Tell when to use pretrained knowledge, when to use web search, and when to use deep research.
  • → Pick the right model/tool for the right task: text, search, research, image, video, audio, data, automation — not "one size fits all".
  • → Use AI to brainstorm, stress-test, and draft — while keeping a real human voice and never letting the output sound hollow.
  • → Work with image, video, audio, and documents — through a verifiable, policy-compliant workflow.
  • → Build mini apps, dashboards, and Agent Skills — automation without heavy coding.
  • → Apply AI to the real processes of your department at Demo.

Before we start

AI changes workflows, not people

Before we get into the technical content, there's one important thing to say clearly. When AI moves as fast as it does now, some people are excited and some are worried. Both feelings are normal — but how Demo sees AI is very clear:

① It doesn't replace skilled people

AI does not replace people who are good at their craft. Real people understand the client, understand the market, and read cultural nuance — AI doesn't have any of that. AI is just a tool that extends your ability.

② It replaces repetitive workflows

AI replaces the repetitive, low-leverage workflows: compiling reports, cleaning data, drafting a first email, mocking a visual concept, tagging mentions. The parts that "eat hours but don't make a difference" — so you can pour your time into what really matters.

③ The Demoian value stays

The value of a Demoian still lies in strategic thinking, client understanding, and the ability to create impact. AI does the rough part, you do the refined part. What makes the difference is judgement: knowing when to trust the output and when to challenge it.

How Demo sees it

Ask the right question. Drop "is AI going to take my job". Ask this instead: "how many low-value hours a week can I free up to do work that creates real impact".

This course is not a "surviving the AI era" survival guide. It's a resource that helps you use AI to do the work you're already good at even better — and to widen the range of what a person at Demo can do.

Module 01 · Foundations

Finding information efficiently with AI

AI has 3 main ways to retrieve information: pretrained knowledge, real-time web search, and deep research. Understand each one so you can pick the right tool.

Lesson 1.1

One-shot asking vs. delegating with verification

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Write a brief with a goal, data, an audience and acceptance criteria.
  • Tell an AI draft apart from a verified result.
  • Respond with specific errors instead of asking it to “do better”.

Two people using the same model can still get very different results. The difference usually lies in how you delegate the work, the data you feed in, the criteria you check against and the feedback loop — not in a secret "magic phrase".

AI Novice

The "non-power" approach

  • → Asks one short question, expects a perfect answer
  • → Gives no context (job, audience, desired format)
  • → Trusts the output 100%, doesn't verify, doesn't follow up
  • → Uses a single model for everything
  • → Gives up after the first failed attempt
AI Power User

The effective approach

  • → Treats AI as a new coworker: needs a brief, needs a feedback loop
  • → Always gives context: who you are, who the audience is, what the output is for
  • → Fixes specific errors until it meets the criteria, not a fixed number of rounds
  • → Picks the model that fits the task (reasoning vs. fast vs. research)
  • → Verifies output with evidence, especially for data/numbers
Mental Model

"AI is a new coworker who doesn't know the context yet"

Picture a coworker who reads fast and writes well but just started on day one. They don't yet know the client, the brand voice, or the team's report format. You need to give the right brief, examples and data; then check the deliverable against clear criteria. A longer brief isn't necessarily better — a brief with the right information is what has value.

Keep the current design system; include loading, empty and error states; write tests for the KPI calculations and run test/lint before reporting done. Don't upgrade dependencies without asking."

Takeaway

Good results start with a brief that has a goal, data and criteria, then go through a loop of verification and specific-error feedback. Treat AI as a new coworker: capable, but not yet aware of the context and not accountable for the final version.

Lesson 1.2

Pretrained Knowledge — What does AI already know?

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Explain why pretrained knowledge is not a database of facts.
  • Separate claims that need a source from brainstorming or format conversion.
  • Build a verification checklist without trusting the confidence the model reports about itself.

A model learns patterns from training data; it is not a database that holds every fact up to a cutoff date. Even old knowledge can be missing or wrong. For information that can change — prices, laws, schedules, who holds a given role — use search/retrieval and check the original source at the moment you're working.

AI is good at
  • ✓ Academic, historical knowledge
  • ✓ Industry best practice (marketing, code, etc.)
  • ✓ Concepts, definitions, frameworks
  • ✓ Code, formulas, languages
  • ✓ Writing, general brainstorming
Be careful with
  • ⚠ Specific numbers (always verify)
  • ⚠ Specific people, companies
  • ⚠ Quotes / citations
  • ⚠ Laws, regulations (may have changed)
  • ⚠ Prices, product specs
Don't use pretrained for
  • ✕ Events after the cutoff date
  • ✕ Today's news
  • ✕ Stock price, exchange rate
  • ✕ Schedules, event calendars
  • ✕ Client/brand-specific information
Hallucination — when AI "makes things up"

When AI doesn't know the answer, it can produce information that sounds very convincing but is wrong. This phenomenon is called hallucination. Every number, name, link and quote AI gives you must be verified — especially in a report for a client.

Sample prompt · Build a list of claims to check 
From the answer above, extract every verifiable claim: numbers, names, dates, quotes, laws, prices, product features.

Return a 4-column table:
Claim | Source currently cited | Original source to find | Status

Status can only be:
- SOURCED: with a link and a directly supporting passage
- NOT SOURCED: needs to be found before use
- INFERENCE: state which facts led to the inference

Do not assign confidence percentages. If you can't open a source, mark it NOT SOURCED.
Takeaway

Use pretrained knowledge to kick-start an answer, not as the final source. Separate the claims that need checking, open the original source, check the date, and clearly mark what is inference.

Quick check

AI says: “The new tax rate, effective last month, is 8%” but cites no source. What do you do?

Lesson 1.3

Web Search — When do you need to look online?

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Recognize which questions are time-dependent and need a web search.
  • Ask for the original link, publish date and effective date instead of just a decorative citation.
  • Know when to skip web search because the task only processes data you already provided.

Many AI apps have a web search mode, but how they search, which sources they reach and how they cite differ by product/plan. Search helps fetch fresh information; it doesn't guarantee a good source or a correct conclusion. Open the link, check the date and author, and see whether the source actually supports the claim.

Turn web search on when
  • → Asking about recent news / events
  • → You need specific, sourced numbers
  • → Market or competitor research
  • → Finding the latest version of a tool or framework
  • → Asking about a specific person, company or product
No web search needed
  • → Writing, brainstorming, refactoring code
  • → Explaining a concept
  • → Translation, format conversion
  • → Summarizing text you've pasted
  • → Roleplay, creative content

Web search = questions that need freshness + a cited source. Pretrained = questions about concepts + skills. Don't use web search for everything — it's slower and sometimes noisier.

Lesson 1.4

Evaluating sources — Source Quality

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Tell an original source, an analysis source and a source that only re-cites apart.
  • Check whether a citation actually proves the claim it's attached to.
  • Separate facts from a source from the inferences of the writer or the AI.

AI search reads a number of web sources and then synthesizes them for you. Which sources the tool uses, which search engine, and how many results it reads can change by platform, account plan and moment. So don't just trust the summary: open the original link, check the publish date, check the author, and prefer the primary source before putting it in a report.

Tier 1 · Trustworthy
  • • Reports from Nielsen, Kantar, eMarketer
  • • Gov sites (gov.vn, sec.gov)
  • • Scientific journals (peer-reviewed)
  • • Leading wires: Reuters, Bloomberg
  • • Official company blogs, IR docs
Tier 2 · Verify further
  • • Mainstream Vietnamese press
  • • Industry blogs with a clear author
  • • Expert Medium, Substack
  • • Wikipedia (as a starting point)
  • • Reddit, Quora (for ideas, not numbers)
Tier 3 · Be careful
  • • SEO content farms
  • • AI-generated blogs (the AI loop)
  • • Aggregated news with no original source
  • • Old forums, Facebook comments
  • • PR pieces not labeled as PR
Sample prompt · Make AI grade its own sources 
Find figures on [the Vietnam e-commerce market over the last 12-24 months] and:
1. List 5 DIFFERENT sources you used (full links).
2. Classify each source into Tier 1/2/3.
3. Mark which sources are primary and which only re-cite (secondary).
4. If the sources conflict on a number → say so and recommend which figure is more trustworthy, with reasons.
5. Finally return one executive-summary paragraph that can be pasted into a Demo ECI report.
Warning · The empty-source loop

When AI searches a popular topic, more and more of the top results are AI-generated content drawn from other AI. This loop creates a "fake consensus". How to check: ask the AI to find the primary source, and don't accept a synthesis from blogs.

Takeaway

Every claim in a Demo client report needs a source + period + sample size. Make AI sort into Tier 1/2/3, dig to the primary source, and don't accept an aggregated number.

An article says “40% of users changed their behavior”, but only links to a press release; the original report and sampling method won't open. What should you write in the report?

Lesson 1.5

Deep Research Mode — When to dig deep

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Write a research contract that spells out the question, scope, timeframe and source standard.
  • Review the research plan before letting AI read and synthesize many sources at once.
  • Receive the output as an evidence table so you can check each important conclusion.

Deep Research is a mode where AI spends more time finding sources, cross-reading, and writing a structured report. It's slower than a normal chat, but useful when a question needs many sources, multiple angles, and clear citations.

Chat
A few seconds
Little or no sourcing needed
Web Search
Fast
A handful of web sources
Deep Research
Noticeably longer
Many queries, many sources

When is Deep Research worth it?

Worth it: an industry landscape report for a client; market sizing; competitive analysis; due diligence before pitching a new industry; a strategic thesis.
Not worth it: a simple question; writing a caption; checking a single fact; brainstorming an idea — a normal chat is faster.
Deep Research Prompt · Industry landscape 
Goal: write a landscape report on the [Beauty & Personal Care market on TikTok Shop Vietnam over the last 12-24 months] for the Demo Media BD team to pitch an FMCG client.

Deep research requirements:
1. Market size for the most recent year, growth rate, and a projection if a trustworthy source exists.
2. Top 10 active brands (both global & Vietnam local).
3. 3 viral content trends (UGC type, hashtag, format).
4. Which KOL/KOC tier is most effective (sub vs mega).
5. Benchmark CPV, CPM, engagement rate for this category.
6. Insight on how consumer behavior differs vs Shopee/Lazada.

Deliverable:
- 200-word executive summary
- ~2,500-word body report split into the 6 sections above
- A comparison table of the top 10 brands
- A full reference list (prefer Tier 1/2 sources)
- Mark every claim without a source as "[needs source]"
Pro Tip · The tighter the brief, the more valuable deep research is

Deep Research runs many steps and usually costs more time/money than a normal chat, so a bad brief amplifies the waste. Before you hit run: (1) review the prompt with the AI itself in normal chat mode ("is this prompt clear enough?"), (2) ask the AI to propose an outline before it digs, (3) confirm the outline, then run.

Takeaway

Deep Research fits landscape reports, industry analysis, due diligence. Brief a full outline, source-quality tiers, and deliverable format — before you hit start. Don't use it for a 5-minute task.

Lesson 1.6 · Lab

Comparing models — Pick the right AI for the task

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Compare models with a set of real tasks and your team's rubric, not just the leaderboard.
  • Balance quality against latency, cost, data rights and integration.
  • Pin the version when you need reproducibility and re-run the eval after every model upgrade.

There's no "best model" — only "the model that fits the task". Because models change fast, the table below is a way to choose by workflow, not a fixed leaderboard. For important work, test 2-3 models on the same real team brief and then decide.

How to keep the model map current yourself

Module 02 · Thinking Partner

AI as a thinking partner

AI is more useful when you use it to expand and test how you think, rather than taking the first answer as the final one. This module covers: brainstorming, context, reasoning, writing — and how to keep AI from "flattering" the user.

Lesson 2.1

Brainstorming — Expanding ideas with AI

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Separate the idea-expansion phase from the phase of choosing and refining ideas.
  • Use specific constraints to create useful differences, not just a long list.
  • Score ideas against criteria tied to real users and real goals.

AI can produce many directions in a short time, but quantity doesn't mean novel or correct. Use it to widen the space of options; you set the criteria, cut the duplicate/stale ideas, check feasibility, and pick the directions worth trying.

The "3-round Brainstorm" formula

  1. 1
    Quantity: "Give me 30 ideas about [X]. Make them varied, including wild ones. One sentence each."
  2. 2
    Pivot: Pick 3 ideas you like. "Take these 3, combine them in pairs, and give me 6 new hybrid ideas."
  3. 3
    Depth: Pick one winner. "Dig into this idea: risks, resources, timeline, KPIs, who'll object, and why."
Sample prompt · Campaign brainstorm for Marketing 
I need to brainstorm content angles for the Tet 2026 campaign of brand [X — a mid-priced carbonated soft drink, targeting Gen Z].

Round 1: give me 25 content angles. Diversity criteria:
- 5 angles about emotion (nostalgia, connection, etc.)
- 5 angles about Northern/Southern/Central Tet culture
- 5 humorous / meme angles
- 5 angles about sustainability / society
- 5 "wild" angles no brand has done yet

Format: number + a one-line tagline + a one-line description of the execution.

BD / Sales

Brainstorm 15 ways to present the same data point — pick the one that fits the personality of the client contact.

Takeaway

Brainstorm = AI handles quantity, you handle quality. Go through 3 rounds: Expand → Hybridize → Deepen. Don't stop at the first answer.

Lesson 2.2

Context — Give the right information at the right time

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Tell instruction, context, example and the data to be processed apart.
  • Provide the smallest slice of information that's still enough to do the job right.
  • Label sources and boundaries so AI doesn't confuse facts with instructions.

Context is often the biggest reason output goes off-topic: the model doesn't know the audience, the real data, the purpose or the constraints unless you provide them. Good context isn't long context; it's the information that is relevant and enough to make the decision, plus how to handle what's still missing.

The C.R.A.F.T framework
C
Context — the situation
R
Role — who you are
A
Audience — the reader
F
Format — the structure
T
Tone — the voice

Write an apology email to the client for the delayed report.

of a large FMCG brand, a 2-year relationship, slightly formal.
The weekly report is 1 day late due to a technical data-source
failure outside our control.
[R] Account Manager, confident but taking ownership.
[A] CMO — busy, skims, hates excuses.
[F] Vietnamese email, 80–120 words, with 1 clear next step.
[T] Professional, no over-apologizing, propose a solution

Demo Context Block · reusable template

Keep it saved in a note app, paste it at the start of every work-related AI chat:

[BACKGROUND]
I work at Demo [subsidiary name], role [role].
Company: a data & insight marketing agency, main products
[SocialPulse / EcomPulse / CommunityHub / SurveyVoice / etc.].

- Clear structure: heading + bullets + table.

Keep this context for every subsequent answer.
Takeaway

A strong prompt = Context + Role + Audience + Format + Tone (C.R.A.F.T). Save a short context block, version it clearly, and keep only what you reuse. Measure the number of edits before/after; when the task changes, update the block instead of pasting old context.

Lesson 2.3

AI Desktop Apps & MCP — When AI works on your machine

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Understand which systems a connector/MCP lets AI read from or act on.
  • Apply least privilege, a clear scope and a confirmation step before sensitive actions.
  • Tell the data AI can see apart from the actions AI is allowed to take.

Web chat is enough for most day-to-day work. Desktop apps, coding agents and connectors are worth it when you need AI to read context from another app, work with files/repos on your machine, or call a tool it has been granted access to. Not every tool can read files, send email or query a database; the real capability depends on the app, plan, connector, and the permissions admins grant.

Web chat

Normal chat

Text conversation, can upload files, generate images. Simple, no setup needed.

Desktop app

ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini app

Handy for shortcuts, screenshots, voice, small files, and working alongside other apps. The specific features vary by platform.

MCP & Agents

Claude Code · Codex · IDE agents

AI reads the repo, proposes file edits, runs commands/tests when allowed, or calls a tool via a connector. Review carefully before it writes files, sends requests, or runs automation.

What is MCP?

Model Context Protocol is an open standard for AI apps to connect to external servers/tools. A server can provide documents, data, or actions like reading a file, finding a ticket, calling an API. AI can only do what the server supports and what the user/admin has allowed. For internal data, start with read access, limit the scope, and have a person review the output.

New trend

AI Agents & automation — from chat to workflows with tools

CONNECTOR / APP
Read the right source

AI reads Drive/Slack/CRM/GitHub through granted access, instead of manual copy-paste. Requires admin approval and access control.

SCHEDULE
Run on a schedule

Daily scans, weekly digests, monthly report drafts. Good for repetitive work, but the output still has to be reviewed by a person before it goes to a client.

ACTIONS
Create files / run code

AI cleans a CSV, makes a chart, drafts a deck, updates a template. Fits when the task has a clear checklist and a way to verify the result.

→ Don't automate too early. Make a prompt template first, use it 5-10 times, measure the ROI and common errors, and only then promote it to a workflow with a connector/schedule.
Applied at Demo · Concrete workflows
▶ Account / Account Manager

If the Gmail/Drive connector has been approved by IT: ask AI to read exactly the folder/thread it's granted, summarize the 5 biggest issues of the week, point to the original email/file, then write a draft weekly update for the account to review before sending.

▶ Research / Insight Analyst

An agent/code tool works on a prepared export folder: read the CSV, run analysis scripts, generate draft charts, write the insight section. The owner still checks the sample, formulas, charts and wording before it goes into the deck.

▶ Tech / Engineering

A coding agent in the repo: "read the project guidelines, look at the PRD in docs/, implement endpoint X, write tests, explain the diff". Use an approval-required mode on real repos; the owner still reviews the code and runs tests before merging.

▶ HR / Admin

With an approved tool: use AI to summarize CVs against the JD and create a draft shortlist. Avoid uploading PII to a public chat; don't let AI reject candidates on its own before an HR person reviews the criteria and results.

Security · Important for Demo

When granting connector/MCP access to client data → grant only the minimum needed. Start read-only if you can. Don't let an AI agent write to or delete production files without a guardrail and approval. Don't paste PII into a public chat. A scheduled workflow must have an owner, logs, and a way to stop it when the output is wrong.

Takeaway

Desktop apps, connectors and agents are only worth it when they reduce copy-paste or help verify a repetitive workflow. Start small: read-only, narrow scope, with a human reviewer. Only allow stronger actions once it's stable.

Lesson 2.4

Reasoning Models — When you need AI to "think hard"

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Recognize which tasks are worth trading extra latency/cost for deeper reasoning.
  • Ask for assumptions, evidence and points of uncertainty instead of forcing out a private chain of thought.
  • Use tools or an independent check for important results.

Models usually answer fast. Reasoning / thinking mode in the major tools spends more processing steps before answering, so it fits tasks with complex constraints. In exchange, it's slower and unnecessary for short writing, simple summaries, or quick brainstorming.

Turn reasoning on when
  • → Logic, math, or complex code problems

Analyze carefully before answering.

Lesson 2.5

Sycophancy — When AI flatters you

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Notice when the way you phrase a question is pulling AI toward agreeing with you.
  • Ask for counter-evidence, the opportunity cost, and the conditions that would make a proposal fail.
  • Separate the proposer's role from the critic's role using a clean context.

AI is trained to keep the user satisfied. But "satisfied" and "correct" don't always overlap. When you show an idea and hint that you like it → AI will praise it. When you ask "this idea is bad, right?" → AI will criticize it. This is sycophancy — the most common ailment of today's AI.

Dangerous for Demo

When you're writing a proposal and ask AI "Is my approach okay?" — you'll easily get praised: "very solid, professional, on the right track" — even when the proposal is weak. You pitch the client, lose the deal, and don't know why.

Questions that trigger sycophancy
  • "Is this idea good?"
  • "Is my plan okay?"
  • "What do you think of this great idea?"
  • "This code is nice and correct, right?"
Questions that force AI to push back
  • "5 reasons this idea could fail."
  • "Critique this plan like a senior consultant."
  • "Who will object, and why?"
  • "Find 3 logic bugs in this code."
Anti-sycophancy template · always paste at the start of an important chat 
Rules for this conversation:

1. DON'T praise my idea just to make me feel good.
2. If you see a logic hole, an information gap, or a wrong assumption → say it directly, without "however" or "you might want to consider".
3. When I propose a plan/idea, default to playing a senior reviewer at a competitor agency — they'll point out the weak spots to win the deal.
4. If I push back weakly, hold your position when you're right. Don't change your mind just because I don't like the answer.
5. When you're not sure, say "not sure" — don't make things up.

Confirm you understand, then I'll paste the content to review.

"Play the client's CMO. Read this proposal, list 5 reasons you would NOT sign."

"Find 3 logical fallacies or cherry-picking in the report below."

"Play a senior staff engineer. List every issue you'd comment on in this code review."

Takeaway

AI defaults to praising you. A power user actively forces AI to push back: use a "senior critic" role, ask for "5 reasons it fails", paste an anti-sycophancy block before reviewing.

Writing with AI — Avoiding hollow prose

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Spot generic phrasing, an even rhythm, and assertions with no evidence.
  • Provide a voice sample, the audience, and observable details to keep it human.
  • Edit against specific criteria instead of just asking it to “write more naturally”.

AI easily falls into an “average-internet” voice: lots of buzzwords, little detail, and an opening about some vague era. A client/boss recognizes it after a few sentences.

Banned list
in the 4.0 era the digital age constantly evolving groundbreaking improvement optimize leverage synergy unlock delve into it's important to note in conclusion — em dash in every sentence "not X — but Y" "not only... but also..."
The Demo voice · aim for this
  • → Short, active sentences
  • → Data first, comment after
  • → Specific headings: "Sales -12% Q4" instead of "Business situation"
  • → Each claim → evidence or an [assumption] tag
  • → One idea per paragraph
  • → Cut redundant adverbs: "very important" → "important"
Style guide block · paste before every writing request 
VOICE: Demo — consultant, evidence-driven, no fluff.

ABSOLUTE RULES:
- Coherent Vietnamese. When keeping English terms, don't inline-translate them if the meaning is already clear.
- Average sentence ≤ 22 words. Avoid sentences with many nested clauses.
- DON'T use: opening with a vague era, "the digital age", "leverage", "unlock", "delve" + "into", "significant", "groundbreaking", "very [adj]", "extremely [adj]", "one could say", "undeniably".
- DON'T open with "In the context of..." or "In the era of...".
- DON'T close with "In summary" / "To sum up" / "Overall".
- Em dash (—) at most once per paragraph, no spamming.
- DON'T use the antithesis formula "Not X — but Y" / "not only X, but also Y" / "It's not just X, it's Y". This is a classic ChatGPT tic; readers spot it instantly and react strongly.

STRUCTURE:
- Lead with the takeaway. The first sentence is the conclusion.
- Each point: 1 claim sentence → 1 evidence/number sentence → 1 so-what sentence.
- Specific headings, with data if available.

Lesson 2.8

Managing context & memory — working with AI over the long haul

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Separate the durable source of truth from the temporary chat history.
  • Create a short checkpoint of decisions, evidence, open items and next steps.
  • Start a fresh session when the old context is noisy, contradictory or too hard to verify.

C.R.A.F.T handles the single prompt. But real work rarely finishes in one line: you go back and forth 20–30 turns for a report, a proposal, a piece of code. The further along you are in the same thread (one continuous chat), the more AI tends to forget the constraints you set at the start, contradict itself, or answer more generically. Steering the whole conversation, not just the opening line, is the habit that takes you from "usable" to "fluent".

Why long chats get "diluted"

Each model can only hold a certain amount of the conversation in "view" for each answer — the working memory for this chat, called the context window. The longer the thread, the harder it is for the model to stay close to the details at the start; anything beyond that limit is lost entirely. This phenomenon is often called context rot: quality drifts as the conversation piles up, even with a model that has a very large context window.

Signs a thread is overloaded
  • → AI repeats what it just said, or contradicts the original instructions
  • → Forgets a constraint you set (format, tone, banned words)
  • → Output gets blander, less anchored to specific details
  • → You have to restate the same context many times
The power user's reflex
  • → One task = one thread. Switch work, open a new chat
  • → When quality drops, reset — don't try to "cure" the old thread
  • → Hand off with a summary, not the whole history
  • → Put recurring context in memory/a project, don't paste it again

Reset or continue?

Continue in the same thread: you're building on the same artifact (one email, one slide, one piece of code) and need AI to remember the decisions just made.
Open a new chat: you're switching to a different task; quality is dropping; the thread is full of trial-and-error branches; or you want AI to "forget" a wrong direction it has clung to for too long.
Technique · Summary handoff — passing over to a new chat 
Before I open a new chat, write a tight "state summary" for me to paste over:

- GOAL: (1 sentence)
- DECIDED: the important decisions (bullets)
- CONSTRAINTS / STYLE: format, tone, banned words (bullets)
- CURRENT DRAFT: paste it verbatim if short, or describe the state
- REMAINING WORK: next steps (bullets)

Write enough that a different AI that hasn't read the history can continue right away.
Long-term memory

Memory & Projects — stop pasting context every time

Many assistants have some form of memory (remembering certain information across chats) and projects/workspaces (grouping files + instructions shared across chats); the names, scope and behavior differ by tool. This is where policy-permitted recurring context goes: role, brand voice, report format — instead of pasting the C.R.A.F.T block every time (see Lesson 2.2).

Data note: don't stuff Tier 3 information (NDA, PII, client figures) into memory — it persists and can leak into another chat. Periodically review what memory has saved, and delete what you don't need (see Lesson 4.1).
Applied at Demo
Account

One thread per client deliverable. Before closing, ask for a state summary and save it to a note so you can pick up next week without losing the thread.

Research

Reset the chat between two different analyses. Don't let insights on brand A bleed into brand B in the same thread.

Marketing

Put the brand guideline + tone in a project/memory once; each campaign only needs its own brief, no need to paste everything again.

Tech

Save the repo conventions + stack in a project. When a code thread gets long and tangled, summarize "what's fixed, what's still broken" and open a clean thread to keep debugging.

Module 2 Takeaway

A good prompt is a sentence skill; managing context is a session skill. One task one thread, reset when it dilutes, hand off with a state summary, put recurring context in memory/a project — and only store sensitive data when both policy and the right product/plan allow it.

Lesson 4.1

AI Safety & Data Privacy — Don't leak client data

L1 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Classify data per internal policy before putting it into AI.
  • Reduce and de-identify data instead of uploading the whole set “for convenience”.
  • Check the data use, retention, region, admin access and third parties of the exact product/plan.

Demo works with hundreds of brands: NDAs, raw mention data, internal financials, employee PII. One prompt in the wrong place can push client data into an unapproved system. Before using AI, classify the data and pick the right account/tool type.

🟢 Tier 1 · Public

Lower risk — still follow policy

  • Publicly available news articles
  • Published brand descriptions
  • Public social posts
  • Generic template docs
🟡 Tier 2 · Internal

Anonymize first

  • Demo internal reports
  • Draft discussion guides
  • Pricing templates (numbers removed)
  • Mention data with the brand hidden
🔴 Tier 3 · Confidential

Only use an approved workflow

  • Client NDAs / contracts
  • Raw client data with a brand name
  • Salaries, HR records, CVs
  • API keys, credentials, passwords
A 3-step rule before pasting
  1. Ask: "If this data hit the news tomorrow, would anyone be harmed?" If yes → Tier 3, stop.
  2. De-identify: Replace brand/people names with pseudonyms and drop unneeded detail. Note: pseudonymization doesn't guarantee anonymity if the remaining attributes can still infer identity.
  3. Pick the right-tier tool: Tier 3 only goes into a company-approved tool/account with clear business/enterprise terms on data usage, retention, audit and admin rights. A Free/Personal plan is NOT ENOUGH.
How to read a policy quickly
  • Business/API/Enterprise usually commits to not training on input/output by default, but you still need to read the retention section and the admin's control rights carefully.
  • Free/Personal can differ in policy by product, country, activity setting, feedback or human review.
  • An unfamiliar AI tool should not be considered safe just because "private" appears on its landing page. It needs IT/Legal review before any client data goes in.
De-identification checklist · run only in an approved tool
Use this checklist only in a company-approved environment for receiving source data:
1. List directly identifying fields and fields that can be combined to infer identity.
2. Keep exactly the fields truly needed for the analytical question; drop the rest.
3. Replace names with stable codes; bucket numbers/locations/times if that precision isn't needed.
4. Export a separate mapping table for the owner to hold; don't send the mapping into the analysis step.
5. Stop and ask the data owner if the policy or processing purpose is unclear.

[Data only goes in here after the tool and permissions are confirmed]

Don't use an unapproved chatbot to “anonymize first”: the moment you paste the source data, it has already left the intended control boundary.

⚠️ Red flags at Demo
  • Pasting a raw SocialPulse export with a real brand → leaks the client's insight.
  • Uploading a contract PDF to free ChatGPT to "summarize it for me" → violates the NDA.
  • Using an unapproved AI tool to rewrite a CV → you don't know the purpose, retention, or which third parties receive the data.
  • Sharing a chat link containing internal data into a wide channel → people outside scope can access it or the link gets reshared.
💡 Takeaway

AI doesn't inherently know which data is okay to send outside — you have to make that call. Before pasting: classify the tier → anonymize → pick the right tool/account. When unsure, ask a leader or IT/Legal first.

Lesson 4.2

Advanced Prompt Patterns — 5 techniques for 5 kinds of problems

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Pick a pattern by the error you need to fix: format, boundaries, criteria, variation or verification.
  • Use varied examples and counter-examples to avoid learning the wrong surface rule.
  • Judge a pattern with an eval instead of trusting a framework name.

C.R.A.F.T is the base framework. When you need to lift the output from "okay" to "usable after review", add the 5 patterns below. Each solves one specific kind of problem.

Pattern 01

Few-shot — Teach AI with examples

When the output needs a specific format or style, give a few varied input → output examples and clearly state which parts are the rule. Then test both common and edge cases; few-shot increases consistency but doesn't guarantee it's always right.

Example applied to the Account team
Write email subject lines to clients in the Demo style. Here are 3 examples:

Input: Weekly social listening report for brand X Output: [SocialPulse] Brand X Weekly Pulse — 3 insights to action

Input: Send Tet campaign pricing proposal Output: [Proposal] Tet 2026 Campaign — Investment & Expected Impact

Input: Reschedule a meeting due to a conflict Output: [Reschedule] [Topic] — Proposing 3 new slots

Now generate one for: "Notify the client about an emerging crisis"
Pattern 02

Reasoning Scaffold — Make AI analyze with structure

Instead of just writing "think step by step", give a clear checklist: which criteria to consider, the order of analysis, and how brief the rationale in the output should be. With a reasoning model, you don't need to force it to show all its hidden reasoning; just ask for a conclusion + evidence + assumptions + a confidence level.

Example applied to the Research team
Analyze whether this viral post (15k shares) is organic or seeded.

Evaluate against this checklist:
1. Engagement pattern: is the like/share/comment ratio unusual?
2. Comment quality: are the first 20 comments copy-paste, same wording, same timing?
3. Timing: do shares grow naturally, or spike in clusters?
4. Account pattern: are there many new accounts, little history, or the same network?

Output:
- Conclusion: organic / likely seeding / not enough data
- The 3 strongest pieces of evidence
- Assumptions or missing data
- Evidence level: strong / medium / weak, with reasons and the data needed to complete it

[Paste data]
Pattern 03

Role + Persona — Make AI play an expert

A role is useful when it points to a perspective and responsibility: “Review the proposal as procurement; prioritize total cost, exit clauses and vendor risk.” A long title or fictional years of experience don't create expertise on their own; pair it with criteria, data and output examples.

Pattern 04

XML Structuring — Separate the input clearly

When a prompt has context, data, instructions and examples, separate them with consistent headings, Markdown or XML. The goal is to make the boundaries visible and keep the model from treating data as instructions; pick whichever syntax the team reads, writes and checks most easily.

A standard template for complex tasks
<context> Demo Media, a social listening agency. Client: [BRAND]. Report audience: Gen Z 18-24 in HCMC. </context>

<data> [Paste 50 top-engagement mentions] </data>

<task> Identify the top 3 conversation themes and the sentiment for each theme. </task>

<format> Markdown table with columns: Theme | Volume | Sentiment | Example quote </format>
Pattern 05

Output Constraint — Force the exact format

Specify exactly the output you want: word count, format, no preamble, no markdown, or a specific JSON schema. AI will stop with the long-winded "Sure, here is...".

Example for the Tech / Data team
Return EXACTLY the following JSON, with no explanation:
{
  "sentiment": "positive" | "neutral" | "negative",
  "confidence": float 0-1,
  "topics": [string],
  "actionable": boolean
}

Input mention: "[paste mention]"
💡 Takeaway

These 5 patterns aren't used all at once — choose by need: Few-shot for format, Reasoning scaffold for analysis, Role for an expert perspective, XML for long prompts, Output Constraint for integration. Combining 2-3 patterns in the right place is usually enough to clearly raise quality.

Lesson 4.3

Integrate AI into the team workflow — From individual to enterprise scale

L2 · ⏱ self-paced
By the end you can
  • Pick a repetitive workflow stable enough and with a baseline before packaging it.
  • Design an owner, reviewer, fallback and logs for the AI steps.
  • Scale by quality metrics and total processing time, not by the number of people who “tried it”.

Value for the whole team only appears when a workflow has an owner, test examples, data guidance and before/after measurement. The four steps below are a suggested rollout process; adjust for your team's risk and way of working.

01

Identify repetitive tasks (≥ 3h/week)

Each team member lists their 5 most time-consuming tasks of the week. Combine them → pick the top 3 tasks with a repeating pattern (report templates, drafting emails, summarizing meetings, formatting data...). This is the first automation target.

02

Build & test a standard prompt template

One person on the team (the AI champion) builds the prompt with C.R.A.F.T + a suitable pattern. Test with 3-5 real cases, tune. Document: expected input, expected output, and which edge cases don't work.

03

Share & train — a workflow doc + a buddy session

Save the prompt in the team's Notion/Confluence. Run a 30-minute buddy session: the AI champion demos, each member runs it once, Q&A. Add it to the onboarding doc for new hires.

04

Measure & iterate — measure with numbers, don't guess

Before/after: task completion time, number of revisions, reviewer satisfaction. Review quarterly. Any workflow that's no longer useful → archive it. Any workflow with high ROI → graduate it to a tool with a UI (build app, Zapier, n8n...).

Governance — Demo's suggested rules
  • ✓ Each team has at least 1 AI champion — who maintains the prompt library and trains new members.

  • ✓ Monthly report: which prompt is used most, time saved, which edge cases fail.
  • ✓ Any new AI tool (beyond Claude/ChatGPT/Gemini Enterprise) must go through IT/Legal review.

A team's productivity comes from documenting, sharing, measuring and updating good workflows. A template used by many people can create leverage, but ROI doesn't grow linearly: there's still the cost of training, review, errors and maintenance.
